Who am I?

My name is Monte Fischer. I was born in South Saint Paul, MN, USA, in one of the concluding years of the twentieth century. After my childhood and public high school education in Minnesota, I headed southwards to study at the University of Georgia in Athens, GA.

I am majoring in Mathematics and Computer Science, both of which have been life-long interests of mine. I started college in August 2016, and I will graduate in May 2020 with a B.S./M.A. in Mathematics and a B.S. in Computer Science.

My education at UGA is paid for (and more) by the UGA Foundation Fellowship. This scholarship has fully funded my travel and study around the world (New York, NY; Washington D.C.; Oxford, UK; Costa Rica; Budapest, Hungary; and more to come) and given me access to a driven and endlessly stimulating community of peers. Being a Fellow is the opportunity of a lifetime, one for which I am very grateful.

I've put together this page as an overview of my skills and interests, a kind of conversational resume. If you are looking for an intern or student researcher and think I might fit the bill, send me an email at monte.fischer@gmail.com.

Spring 2020 is my last semester at UGA! I'm working on reconstructing video using neural networks for my master's thesis, with Dr. Alexander Petukhov as my advisor.

I spent summer 2019 in New York City, interning at the Federal Reserve (all views on this site my own, etc.) for the summer. I worked in Research under Dr. Michael Lee of the Money and Payment Studies function.

Before this, I spent the fall of my junior year studying abroad in Budapest at the Budapest Semester in Mathematics program. BSM plunges promising American undergraduates into the rich mathematical tradition of Hungary by means of some truly outstanding (and difficult) classes taught by world-renowned mathematicians. I took 4 hard math/CS classes, got to know some of the brightest of my peers, and explored Budapest. I had my sights on this program since high school, and was fulfilling to finally go.

Before this, I finished my sophomore year and did applied math research at Worcester Polytechnic Institute with a industry-sponsored / NSF-funded financial mathematics research group for the summer. When I wasn't working I am probably walking around Worcester, MA (the second-largest city in New England that, somehow, is completely unknown outside of New England) or walking around Boston, MA, which quickly became one of my favorite cities.

Research and Professional Interests
  • Analysis / Probability / Financial Mathematics
  • Discrete Mathematics / Computer Science
  • Things That Work Well to Do Good Things

I began college a pure mathematician, but have moved towards applications in terms of career outlook. Though I continue to take plenty of analysis and combinatorics classes, the financial mathematics research project I worked on in the summer of 2018 helped me understand the appeal and virtue of applied mathematics, and got me highly interested in the financial world.

I wrote my first computer program at age 11, and read my first graph theory book at age 16. More recently, I traveled to Budapest to study combinatorics with some of the greatest figures in the field. The modern world is inextricably tied to computing and the mathematics behind it, and I don't intend to be left behind.

I like things that are reliable, and I especially like reliable things that deliver positive value. Stated negatively, I dislike bloated things that drain human vitality and spirit. I want to give the world more of the first kind of thing, and avoid filling the world with the second kind of thing. I am intrigued by the use of quantitative thinking to improve people's physical or mental well-being. Examples: optimizing emergency response vehicle placement in a town; developing biomedical imaging technology; creating quality, reliable software.
